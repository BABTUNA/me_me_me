The best tool I built last year was 80 lines of Python. It does one thing.
It does it fast. I use it every day.

I keep meaning to "make it a proper project" — add config, plugins, tests.
Every time I try, the tool gets worse.

## The case for small

A small tool is legible. You can read it in one sitting, hold it in your
head, change it without ceremony. Bugs surface immediately because there
isn't anywhere for them to hide.

A small tool is also honest. It doesn't pretend to be more than it is.
It doesn't ask you to learn a framework just to do a thing.

## When small breaks down

Small breaks when the problem genuinely *is* big. A scheduler isn't a
script. A database isn't a script. Don't write a small tool when you need
a system.

But most of the time, you don't need a system. You need a script. The
mistake is reaching for the system reflexively.

## A rule of thumb

> If you can't explain what the tool does in one sentence, it's already
> too big.

I'm trying to write more small things. Most of them won't be a big deal.
That's fine. The point is the small.
